Week One Summary

Sep 20th, 2013

The first week of the Fullstack course has come to a close; taking the course is making me learn to use tools that I hadn’t gotten around to using before: Git, blogging tools, rspec. We started the course with a basic exercise in HTML and CSS, building a personal website based on an image. Uploading our solutions to a unified repository became an interesting exercise in the use of Git. After that we reviewed (and completed) basic Ruby exercises from the pre-term assignment, but the most interesting thing we did in the first week was probably setting up a blog on Octopress.

The recommended format for composing blogs on Octopress is a simple markup language called Markdown, which is preprocessed into HTML. What’s distinctive about Markdown is its simplicity and elegance: the source file looks almost exactly like plain text and the formatting commands are unobtrusive, yet it supports the most important HTML elements to use in structuring text. I’ve used Markdown to reformat the private journal that will form the basis for this blog, and will continue to write in this format.

The biggest challenge I’ve experienced so far has been associated with the commute. Before the course started I knew how to get to Fullstack by taking New Jersey Transit into Manhattan (Penn Station) and then taking the subway. Riding New Jersey Transit is generally pleasant—I sit down, get out my iPad, and have time to watch web development tutorials from Team Treehouse. In the middle of the week, however, I decided to try the PATH train. Three days ago I didn’t even know what the PATH train is, but on Tuesday morning I happened to be looking out the window on the left side of the train when we stopped in Newark Penn Station (can’t they give the stations more distinctive names?); and I saw that there were turnstiles leading to a light rail train labeled “Port Authority Trans Hudson”, heading to the World Trade Center. I’d been to New York before and seen signs to PATH, but none of those signs had informed me that those signs lead to a train line to New Jersey. It was by looking out the the window that, absurdly late, I learned that there is a light rail from New Jersey direct to downtown Manhattan.

That night, to try out the PATH train, I took the subway to the Park Place stop where the signs and announcements said “connection is available to the PATH train”, found the station, and went down to the tracks. The monitor there announced, “Due to signal problems, the trains between WTC and Newark in both directions are subject to a 10 minute delay.” I took the train to Newark, got out, spent several minutes wandering through the train station (those readers who are familiar with the station already know that this reflects a serious mistake) until I found the track, just after missing a train, and waited fifteen minutes for the next train.

Last night I tried the PATH train again. This time I walked to WTC, and when I got there the monitors said, “Due to a sick passenger, the trains between WTC and Newark in both directions are subject to a 10 minute delay.” The train I boarded had twenty minutes of accumulated passengers, and was very full; the next one was already on the track opposite, and was almost empty. After 25 minutes of being squeezed like a sardine on that train, I got off and saw a corridor: To Westbound Connecting Trains. It takes you straight to the platform that I had taken so long to find two nights before! If I’d seen it two nights before, I wouldn’t have missed the train. Getting back to my story, I didn’t get on the first train, because it was heading to Jersey Avenue and I didn’t know where that was. Instead, I waited for the first Northeast Corridor train to Trenton. Once I was on, I discovered I was on the wrong train: it was an express train, next stop Princeton Junction. I watched the train go past New Brunswick, go past Jersey Avenue (oh, that’s where it is) and continue on. My wife decided to drive to Princeton to pick me up, and so I spent almost an hour at the station waiting for her. It was four hours after leaving Fullstack that I finally got home.
